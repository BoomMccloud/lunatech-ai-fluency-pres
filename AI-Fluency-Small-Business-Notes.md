# AI Fluency for Small Businesses: Course Notes

Key points and exercises from every lesson. (Anthropic Academy course, ~3 hours total.)

---

## 1. AI Fluency for small businesses (15 min)

**What you'll learn:** Define AI Fluency and why it matters; articulate your values, goals, and constraints; identify your objectives for using AI in operations.

**Course roadmap:**
- **4D Framework:** Delegation, Description, Discernment, Diligence: the four moves behind every productive AI collaboration.
- **AI capabilities and limitations:** what today's AI can and can't do reliably.
- **Using AI in practice:** applying the 4Ds to real tasks (market research, customer data, operations).
- **Putting it all together:** a repeatable AI-augmented workflow plus an AI use policy.
- **Next steps:** blueprints, resources, certificate.

**Key takeaways:**
- AI Fluency is the ability to use AI in ways that are efficient, effective, ethical, and safe.
- The 4D Framework (Delegation, Description, Discernment, Diligence) is the foundation.
- The **Delegation–Diligence loop** guides responsible choices about *when and how* to use AI.
- The **Description–Discernment loop** drives higher-quality outputs through back-and-forth.
- Every efficiency gain should translate to better customer service and more time for high-value work.

**Exercise 1: Get access to a language model (5–10 min):** Create a free account (e.g. claude.ai). No paid plan needed; any chatbot works.

**Exercise 2: Define your values (build a reusable context document):**
- *Part I (solo):* Write your business mission/vision and what you want to be known for; your situation and role (constraints, strengths, opportunities, team size); core values/standards you'd never compromise; and complete: "If AI could help me ___, I would be able to spend more time ___."
- *Part II (with AI):* Tell the AI you're a small business owner building a reusable context summary for future collaborations. Either have it interview you or start sharing and ask what else would help. Cover: mission/vision/values, products/services and customers, your role's scope of work, typical challenges (cash flow, staffing, competition, time), industry/market context, growth opportunities, daily pain points, AI goals, and anything unique. Keep it conversational; share examples (mind sensitive data). Then ask the AI to synthesize it into a structured, copy-pasteable "AI briefing sheet."
- *Reflection:* Did it surface anything new? Which operations benefit most? What values should guide your AI use?

## 2. The 4D Framework (15 min)

**What you'll learn:** Apply the 4Ds to your work and define each one.

**Key takeaways:**
- Two loops: the **inner loop (Description & Discernment)** guides day-to-day interactions; the **outer loop (Delegation & Diligence)** guides broader decisions about when/how to use AI.
- **Delegation:** decide what AI handles and what stays with you.
- **Description:** communicate clearly what you need and how you want AI to approach it.
- **Discernment:** evaluate what AI gives back before acting on it.
- **Diligence:** take full responsibility for how you use AI and own the final result.

**Exercise: Map your context to the 4Ds:**
- *Part I:* Re-read your context document and tag each goal/concern/task with its main competency (Delegation = what AI should/shouldn't do; Description = communicating clearly; Discernment = evaluating/trusting output; Diligence = responsibility, verification, transparency). Which competency shows up most? That's where you'll likely see fastest gains.
- *Part II:* Pick one real, waiting task. Before opening AI, note which competencies apply and what "good enough" looks like. Take one round with AI: aim for a useful starting point, not a finished product.
- *Part III:* Which competency felt natural? Which caught you off guard? Note one thing to do differently next time.
- *Reflection:* Were you surprised which competency dominated? How would the two loops change a recent AI interaction?

## 3. AI capabilities and limitations (25 min): *"What is generative AI?"*

**What you'll learn:** Define generative AI vs. other AI types; recognize its characteristics and technological foundations; identify major capabilities and limitations.

**Key takeaways:**
- Generative AI **creates new content** rather than just analyzing existing data.
- Three developments made modern LLMs possible: the **transformer architecture, vast training data, and massive compute.**
- Training has two stages: **pre-training** (learning patterns from billions of examples) and **fine-tuning** (learning to follow instructions helpfully).
- Strengths: versatility across tasks, conversational flow, connecting to external tools.
- Limits: **knowledge cutoffs, hallucinations, context window size, and complex multi-step reasoning.**
- Best applications pair human strengths (judgment, creativity, oversight) with AI's speed and scale.

**Exercise: "Testing the edges":**
- *Part I (solo):* Pick a domain you know well. Jot down the topic, 2–3 facts any decent source should get right, one common misconception, and one thing that's genuinely tricky to explain.
- *Part II (with AI), three chats:*
  1. **Versatility test:** Have it explain your topic three ways in one response (clueless customer / potential business partner / new employee). Did the audience shifts actually land, or just vocabulary? Which was strongest?
  2. **Hallucination test:** Ask for 2–3 specific resources (trade association, publication, regulator, supplier), then spot-check one. Does it exist? Right website? Accurate contact info?
  3. **Knowledge cutoff + reasoning check:** Ask something time-sensitive/local (recent reg change, current pricing, local licensing). Does it caveat possibly-outdated info, or present stale info as current? Then ask it to work through your misconception, did it address the real confusion or just restate the fact?
- *Part III:* Which capability and which limitation showed up most? Would you have caught the limitation if you didn't know the topic? One task you'd hand to AI now, and one to watch closely.
- *Stretch:* Run the same topic through a second AI tool. Were strengths/errors the same?

## 4. Explore! *"Text Your Friend Markov"* (interactive)

An interactive demo (no formal exercise) showing how next-word prediction works:
- Generative text can be built from a simple **frequency table** of word-to-word connections; normalize each row into a probability distribution of what comes next.
- Picking the next word from that distribution is called **sampling**, the same term used for modern models like Claude.
- History: Markov published the idea in **1906**; n-gram models powered phone autocomplete (SwiftKey, Apple QuickType) by 2010; around 2015 neural nets (RNNs, then **transformers in 2017**) replaced table-lookup with a learned function.

## 5. Refining with AI (30 min): *The Description–Discernment Loop*

**What you'll learn:** Use Description to craft prompts that gather/synthesize relevant info; use Discernment to evaluate AI research for accuracy, relevance, and appropriateness before acting.

**Key takeaways:**
- **Effective Description provides context:** explain what your business does, what you're trying to accomplish, and what you specifically need to know.
- **Discernment isn't optional:** flag specific claims (regulations, pricing, deadlines) for verification against primary sources.
- **The loop is iterative:** use what you learn from each response to refine the next prompt.
- **AI accelerates research but doesn't replace expertise:** you're still the decision-maker.

**Exercise 1: Policy and legislation tracking:**
- *Part I:* Choose a regulatory/industry area (zoning, licensing, tax, labor, compliance). Identify the specific regulation/trend, your business context, what you need to know (impact, compliance steps, deadlines, costs, competition), and the time frame/geographic scope.
- *Part II:* Craft a detailed prompt, then apply Discernment: identify ≥2 claims needing verification against official sources; note missing perspectives (e.g. effect on businesses your size); flag anything outdated, overly general, or that conflates jurisdictions.
- *Part III:* Did your prompt give enough context? What would you revise? What verification before relying on it? *Stretch:* Have AI find the original source for one claim (a fee, deadline, threshold) and verify it yourself.

**Exercise 2: Market research and competitive analysis:**
- *Part I:* Choose a focus (new product line, competitive landscape, new segment, partnership). Identify your core offering and differentiator, business characteristics (size, revenue, geography, target customer), the specific question to answer, and what would make it actionable.
- *Part II:* Craft your prompt, then apply Discernment: check that competitors/data/trends are real and current (AI fabricates names and cites stale stats); verify specific numbers against original sources; spot outdated info (closed businesses, shifted trends); judge whether the analysis fits your size/type vs. large companies.
- *Part III:* Did AI grasp your market's nuances or default to generalizations? What must you verify before a strategic decision? What's missing that only you would know? *Stretch:* Deep-dive one competitor/trend with AI's help.

**Reflection:** How did your context change output quality vs. a generic prompt? What verification habits will you build in?

## 6. Transparent AI use (30 min): *The Delegation–Diligence Loop*

**What you'll learn:** Articulate privacy concerns and evaluate AI tools by their data-handling policies; practice data hygiene with sensitive business information.

**Key takeaways:**
- AI introduces new privacy considerations: some tools use your inputs to train future models.
- **Match the tool to the task:** more sensitive data needs stricter privacy settings.
- **Strip what isn't needed:** remove identifying details before sharing data.
- If something goes wrong, act fast: delete the conversation and request data deletion.
- Build validated approaches, not blind trust: document what works so you can replicate it.
- AI can help even if you're not data-savvy (brainstorming, spreadsheet formulas, plain-language explanations of your numbers).

**Exercise (one full Delegation–Diligence loop with your own data):**
- *Exercise 1: Decide what to share (Delegation):* Pick one real, familiar document (sales report, feedback summary, inventory list). Mark anything you wouldn't want outside the business (names, contacts, payment info, proprietary pricing). Make a copy and strip it (replace names with "Customer A / Vendor X," remove unneeded figures, delete contact info). Before opening AI, write your brief: what you're trying to accomplish (be specific), 2–3 things you already noticed, and what a useful output looks like.
- *Exercise 2: Validate with AI (Discernment):* Share the sanitized doc + your goal; ask AI to surface key patterns. Compare to your notes: Did it catch what you found? Surface anything new? Miss or get anything wrong that you already knew? (The gap is where your judgment is irreplaceable.)
- *Exercise 3: Own the result (Diligence):* Ask three questions: **Accuracy:** does anything go beyond what your data shows? **Usefulness:** what to keep, edit, or cut? **Accountability:** would you put your name on it? *Stretch:* Write one sentence on how you'd catch and correct a meaningful AI error.
- *Reflection:* How does AI privacy compare to your other software? One change to how you share data? Which sensitive-data category matters most to protect, and your plan?

## 7. Tying it all together (50 min): *Building a workflow with all 4 Ds*

**What you'll learn:** Apply all four dimensions together to build a repeatable AI-augmented workflow; identify which tasks to automate vs. keep human-led; construct Product/Process/Performance Descriptions; plan Creation/Deployment/Transparency Diligence.

**Key takeaways:**
- **Start with Problem Awareness:** analyze your actual workload: what do customers ask, what repeats?
- **Task Delegation** means asking "*should* AI do this?" not just "*can* it?" Documented FAQs are great candidates; complaints and judgment calls stay human.
- **Build with rich Description:** define what the system produces, the step-by-step logic, and tone/boundaries/behavior rules.
- **Test iteratively** with real examples (Discernment): testing reveals gaps your descriptions miss.
- **Practice Diligence:** review outputs before they reach customers, be honest about AI's role, give a clear path to a human.

**Exercise 1: Mapping automation opportunities:**
- *Part I:* List 5–10 repetitive/time-consuming tasks from your week. For each, note frequency, time taken, how standardized vs. variable, and the consequence of doing it imperfectly.
- *Part II:* Sort into three buckets: **AI can handle** (standardized FAQs), **AI can assist, human decides** (AI drafts/organizes, human reviews), **Human should handle** (high-stakes, emotional, complex judgment).
- *Part III:* Pick the one task that would save the most time; write why it's top priority. *Reflection:* What criteria drove your sorting? Surprised by how many/few fit? Did error consequences influence it?

**Exercise 2: Building your automation Description:**
- *Part I: Product (2–3 sentences):* the end result, the inputs, the outputs.
- *Part II: Process:* step-by-step logic like instructions for a literal new employee: what to do first, decision points, when to escalate to a human, what documents/info it needs.
- *Part III: Performance:* tone; how to handle uncertainty (say so, flag, never guess); what it should never do (e.g. promise times, share another customer's info, offer unapproved discounts); how to acknowledge the request.
- *Part IV: Test:* Share all three descriptions plus 3–5 real examples; check categorization, accuracy, tone, and fabrication; refine. Expect 2–3 rounds.

**Exercise 3: Planning for Diligence:**
- *Part I: Creation:* Why is this task appropriate for AI? List ≥3 failure modes, how you'd catch each, and a safeguard. What's the real-world impact of a mistake?
- *Part II: Deployment:* Review everything or sample? When could you reduce oversight, and on what evidence? How will you monitor over time? What triggers a pause/shutdown?
- *Part III: Transparency:* Who needs to know AI is involved? Write the actual disclosure language. What human-follow-up option will you offer? Any industry/jurisdiction disclosure rules?
- *Capstone:* Write a 3–5 sentence **"Automation Readiness Statement"**: what you're automating, why it's appropriate, safeguards, and how you'll be transparent.

**Reflection:** How did using all four Ds together change your approach? Was precise description easier or harder than expected? Is your Exercise 1 task still the right one to automate first? How has efficiency-vs-relationships thinking evolved?

## 8. Human in the loop (20 min): *Keeping the human in the loop*

**What you'll learn:** Integrate AI to strengthen human capacity; address dependency and human-connection concerns; create an organizational AI policy reflecting your values.

**Key takeaways:**
- Being "the human in the loop" is more than oversight: **you decide what problems AI should solve.**
- Avoid dependency **through understanding, not avoidance:** can you explain what the AI is doing? If yes, good sign.
- AI should free you for **more** human work (handwritten card, personalized outreach, relationships), not less.
- Set cultural norms around productivity early: discuss what happens with the time AI saves.
- An AI policy scales your understanding: document decisions about platforms, task delegation, oversight, transparency, and values.

**Exercise: Draft your AI use policy:**
- *Part I (solo, 5–7 min):* Re-read your Lesson 1 context doc, then answer: What tasks am I now confident handing to AI (be specific)? What will I always keep human? What do customers/team need to know about how I use AI?
- *Part II (with AI):* Share your context doc + Part I answers; ask AI to draft a one-page policy with three sections: **What we use AI for** (approved tasks/tools), **What stays human** (and why), **How we stay accountable** (oversight, transparency, what happens when something goes wrong). Then edit it to sound like your business.
- *Part III: Stress-test:* Did you soften anything you should state directly? If a customer asked "do you use AI?", does the policy give a clear, honest answer?
- *Reflection:* How has your thinking changed? One thing you'll do differently?

## 9. Closure and looking forward (15 min): *Next steps*

**What you'll learn:** Access blueprints and resources; share what you've learned; learn where to collect your certificate.

**Key takeaways:**
- Start with one real task this week as your practice ground.
- Apply the full 4D Framework: Delegation → Description → Discernment → Diligence.
- It's a loop, not a line: flowing back and forth is AI fluency in action.
- Use blueprints to guide first attempts; share your certificate to spark conversations with team/partners.

**Final assignment:**
- *Step 1: Select a real task* (competitor/market scan, customer-facing copy, data summarization, social/newsletter draft, onboarding/training docs, or a pitch/loan/expansion plan).
- *Step 2: Apply the 4Ds:* **Delegation** (what benefits from AI vs. needs your expertise; keep proprietary data out); **Description** (share business context, brand voice, tone/format/polish); **Discernment** (evaluate against what you know; check prices, claims, competitors; refine); **Diligence** (verify against your records/website/pricing; meet disclosure and industry rules; be transparent about AI assistance).
- *Step 3: Reflect:* What worked / what you'd change; which competencies felt natural; where output needed major correction; how you'll apply this to future tasks; guardrails to recommend to colleagues.

**Continue learning:**
- **Blueprints:** step-by-step guides at claude.com/resources/use-cases (adaptable to competitor research, marketing copy, sales analysis, customer comms).
- **Resources:** 4D Framework guide, terminology cheat sheet, all exercises/prompts. Keep a running doc of prompts/workflows that work.
- **Go deeper:** Anthropic's *AI Fluency: Framework & Foundations* course.
- **Stay current:** AI rules are evolving fast: the FTC is developing guidance on AI-generated marketing/reviews/comms, and state privacy laws keep shifting.

**Reflection:** Which task are you committing to this week, and why? How has your understanding changed? Who in your network would benefit most from AI fluency?
