🧑‍💼 The setup

Meet Maya. She's underwater.

Maya is a marketing coordinator. Every morning she spends 90 minutes reading industry news so she can email her team a summary.

  • By the time she's done, her actual work hasn't started.
  • It's the same chore, every single day.
  • She dreads it — but the team relies on it.
Takeaway
AI is for the real, repetitive work you already have — not science projects.
🧑‍💼 The setup

Her task: a daily industry newsletter

Not every job is a good fit for AI. A great first task passes a simple test — the 4 R's:

Repetitive — happens often Rule-ish — has a pattern Reviewable — you'd spot a mistake Reversible — low stakes to try

Maya's newsletter checks all four. That's why it's the perfect place to start.

Task #1
List out some items from your own work that fall under the 4 R's.

Under the hood

🔒 Mostly fixed — your job here is to  understand
Takeaway
Know the machine before you drive it.
① Under the hood · first contact

Attempt #1: she asks a chatbot

Maya types "summarize today's industry news" into a chatbot. She gets back:

  • A generic paragraph — no links, no sources.
  • Nothing specific to her industry.
  • One "fact" she's pretty sure is wrong.

Helpful-ish — but why did it do that? Let's look under the hood.

Takeaway
A chatbot answers a question; understanding why it behaves this way is step one.
① Under the hood · the brain

Why it knew some things — but not today's

Behind the chatbot is a model — a "brain" trained by reading enormous amounts of text.

🚗 Think of a driver who learned the city a year ago: they know the main roads, but not the street that opened last week. That date is the model's knowledge cutoff.

Takeaway
A model is a brain trained on past text — it doesn't automatically know what's new.
① Under the hood · how it "thinks"

Why it sounded so sure — even when wrong

The model works by predicting the next most likely word, over and over. That's what makes it fluent and confident.

It also means it can state a made-up fact just as smoothly as a true one. That wrong "fact" Maya spotted? This is exactly why it happens.

Takeaway
It predicts likely words — so confidence is not the same as being correct.
① Under the hood · what it can sense

Maya pastes a screenshot. Can it see?

It depends on the model:

Text-only

Reads words only. Hand it an image and it's blind — like a driver following written directions.

Multimodal

Can also see pictures & charts (some can hear too) — like a driver who can see the road.
Takeaway
Only multimodal models can "see" images; text-only ones read words only.
Part two
02

Tools & skills

How a generic brain becomes your assistant. This is the layer you actually plug in, connect, and customize.

🔧 Hands-on — this is where  you customize
Takeaway
The model is the same for everyone; what you connect to it is what makes it yours.
② Tools & skills · reaching out

She needs today's news — give it tools

A brain alone can't fetch anything. Tools are the instruments it can use to reach the outside world:

🔎 search the web 📄 open a file ✉️ send an email 📅 check a calendar

With a web-search tool, the model can pull today's headlines instead of guessing from memory. You choose which tools to connect.

Takeaway
Tools let AI reach beyond its memory — your files, the web, your inbox.
② Tools & skills · assembling an agent

Put a brain inside a car (the harness — software like Cowork) and hand it instruments, and you get an agent: give it a goal and it plans the steps, uses the tools, and adjusts — like a chauffeur who just drives you to the airport.

Takeaway
An agent takes a goal and does the multi-step work — a chatbot just talks.
② Tools & skills · making it repeatable

She writes it down so it repeats — a skill

The first run works — but Maya doesn't want to re-explain it every morning.

So she saves the instructions as a skill: a documented, reusable routine — "how we build the newsletter." Define it once, and the agent runs it again and again. This is the most powerful thing you'll customize.

Takeaway
A skill is a saved routine: define the task once, reuse it forever.
Part three
03

How to use it well

The habits that turn a capable agent into real, safe value — and keep you in control. This is where the payoff lives.

⭐ The real value — this is  all you
Takeaway
The tool is only as good as the judgment of the person steering it.
③ How to use it · the 4 Ds

Should AI do this part? — Delegation

Before handing off, Maya splits the task:

  • AI does the legwork — scanning sources, drafting, formatting.
  • Maya keeps the editorial voice and the call on sensitive stories.
  • She only feeds it public news — never confidential data.
Takeaway
Delegation = hand off the legwork, keep the judgment (and protect private data).
③ How to use it · the 4 Ds

Tell it what "good" looks like — Description

"Summarize the news" gives mush. Maya describes the job in three layers:

ProductA 5-story, ~300-word newsletter, each with a link.
ProcessScan these 6 sources → drop dupes → rank by relevance.
PerformanceSharp but friendly; never invent a source; flag if unsure.
Takeaway
The clearer you describe the job, the better the result — every time.
③ How to use it · the 4 Ds

Read the draft like an editor — Discernment

Maya doesn't just skim — she checks:

  • Are the links real?
  • Is anything stale or out of date?
  • Is it specific to her industry, or generic filler?

Each fix she makes — "never summarize an opinion piece as news" — she adds back into the skill.

Takeaway
Always judge the output before you trust it — and feed your fixes back in.
③ How to use it · the 4 Ds

Maya signs off — Diligence

When the newsletter goes out, it goes out under Maya's name. So she:

  • Reviews it before sending.
  • Notes that AI helped, where that matters.
  • Has a plan to correct a mistake fast.
Takeaway
You own whatever ships under your name — review, disclose, be ready to fix.
③ How to use it · the rhythm

It's a loop, not a line

Maya's first draft wasn't perfect — and that's completely normal.

After a few rounds, the skill is reliable.

Takeaway
Great AI work is a loop: describe, judge, refine — repeat until it's right.
③ How to use it · how far to go

Co-pilot or autopilot?

There are two ways to use an agent:

Co-pilot

AI does the legwork; a human approves. Where Maya starts. Safe, fast wins.

Autopilot

The agent runs the whole loop alone — e.g. customer service that answers and refunds. More power, more risk.
Takeaway
Start as co-pilot; move a task to autopilot only once it's earned trust.
③ How to use it · how far to go

She schedules the draft (not the send)

Maya sets the agent to build the newsletter every morning at 6am and drop a draft in her folder.

She still hits publish herself at 8 — until she's seen enough good drafts to trust it with more.

Takeaway
Schedule the draft, not the send — keep a human on the publish button.
③ How to use it · where it nearly went wrong

Near-miss #1: a link that goes nowhere

One draft cited "a TechCrunch article" — but the link 404'd. The model had invented a plausible-sounding source.

Maya caught it only because she checks. This is called a hallucination.

Takeaway
AI can invent sources that look real — verify before you rely on them.
③ How to use it · where it nearly went wrong

Near-miss #2: the data she almost pasted

Maya nearly dropped a confidential customer list into the tool to "personalize" the newsletter. She stopped herself.

  • Never feed an AI tool private or proprietary data you wouldn't want stored.
  • And remember: bad inputs → bad outputs. Garbage in, garbage out.
Takeaway
Mind your inputs: don't share private data, and garbage in means garbage out.
③ How to use it · where it nearly went wrong

Near-miss #3: yesterday's news as today's

Without a live web tool, the agent leaned on its training memory and served up an "update" that was months old.

For a newsletter, that's fatal. The fix: give it live sources and tell it to flag anything time-sensitive.

Takeaway
For anything time-sensitive, give AI live sources — don't trust its memory.
③ How to use it · the payoff

Maya, 90 days later

The newsletter runs itself every morning. Maya spends 10 minutes reviewing instead of 90 writing.

  • She's spent the reclaimed time on campaigns only she can do.
  • And she's built two more skills the same way.
Takeaway
Fluency compounds: each skill you build frees time for work only you can do.
