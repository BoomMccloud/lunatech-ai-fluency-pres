2 · The basics: one car, one chauffeur

Goal: Everyone can define model, harness, tools, skills, agent, and why the distinctions matter in practice. Build the analogy piece by piece.

Modelthe brain
The driving intelligence. Raw capability: judgment, language, reasoning. On its own it can think, but it can't move anything yet. Models also have a knowledge cutoff (it learned the city as it was a year ago, new roads aren't on its mental map) and it works by predicting the most likely next move (so it can take a confident wrong turn).
Harnessthe car
The car itself: chassis, dashboard, pedals. The brain can't drive without a body. The harness is the software that wraps the model so it can take real steps and reach the controls. Claude Code and Cowork are harnesses.
Toolsthe instruments
GPS, fuel gauge, wipers, the phone link. Specific things the car can use to reach outside itself: look up a route (web search), check the tank (read a file), make a call (send an email). Tools are how an agent touches your files, the web, your calendar.
Skillsthe routes
Learned routes & maneuvers: "the route to the airport," "how to parallel park." A skill is a documented, reusable procedure: e.g., "how we build the newsletter." Define it once, reuse it forever.
Agentthe chauffeur
Put it together. You say "take me to the airport." The chauffeur, brain (model) driving the car (harness), reading the GPS & gauges (tools), following known routes (skills), handles the whole trip and adjusts to traffic, without you narrating every turn. That's an agent: give it a goal, it plans, executes multiple steps, and self-corrects.

The one nuance to call out: not all brains see the same

Text-only model: a driver who can only follow written turn-by-turn directions. Hand it a photo of a road sign or a scanned PDF and it's stuck.
Multimodal model: a driver who can also see the road through cameras (and hear). It can read your screenshot, your scanned invoice, your chart.
💡 Memorable line"A chatbot answers a question. An agent takes the wheel."
🎤 Say this"Why care about these five words? Because when something goes wrong, you'll know where: a bad answer is the brain; can't open your file is a missing tool; does the same task inconsistently means you never wrote the skill down."

3 · How agents create value in a business

Goal: Two modes, one spectrum, and where to start.

① Augment a human: co-pilot

The agent does the legwork; a person steers and signs off. our example The newsletter: AI scans sources, drafts, and formats; you pick the angle and hit publish. This is where most safe, fast wins live.

② Replace a task: autopilot

The agent runs the whole loop with little human touch, e.g., agentic customer service: read the ticket, look up the order, issue the refund, reply. Higher leverage, higher risk; needs guardrails, monitoring, and a clear path to a human.
  • It's a spectrum, not a switch. Start in the co-pilot's seat, earn trust, then hand over narrow, well-bounded routes.
  • The real question isn't "can it do this?" It's "should it, and how much rope do I give it?"
💡 Memorable line"Co-pilot vs. autopilot. Start in the co-pilot's seat; earn your way to autopilot one route at a time."
🙋 Quick think (20 sec)"Picture one task from your week. Is it a co-pilot job or an autopilot job, and why? Hold that thought; we'll decide it properly with the 4 Ds."
🎤 Say this"Agentic customer service is real and powerful, but notice it's the deep end. Today we're teaching you to swim in the shallow end first, because that's where you get value this week without risk."

5 · Inside the loops newsletter

Goal: Go deep on the daily mechanics with our anchor example.

Inner loop: Description ⇄ Discernment

Description = instructions for a brilliant, literal chauffeur who's never driven your town. Write it in three layers:

ProductA daily ~300-word newsletter, 5 stories on [our industry], each a headline + link.
ProcessScan these 6 sources → drop duplicates → 2-sentence summary each → rank by relevance to our customers → format.
PerformanceTone: sharp but friendly. Never invent a source or stat. Always include the link. Unsure → flag it, don't guess.

Discernment = read it like an editor, not a reader. Every correction ("don't summarize opinion pieces as news") folds back into the skill. That's the loop turning.

💡 Memorable contrast"Summarize today's news" → generic mush. The three-layer version → something you'd actually send. Same model, 10× the output; the difference is the description.

Outer loop: Delegation ⇄ Diligence

  • Delegation: AI scans, drafts, formats; the human keeps the editorial voice and the call to feature a sensitive story. Right data = a public news scan (low-risk); don't feed it confidential customer info.
  • Diligence: review before send, "schedule the draft, not the send," disclose AI's role where it matters, earn-down oversight as trust grows, and have a correction plan for the day a wrong fact slips out.
📰 Memorable line"Schedule the draft, not the send." The agent builds the issue at 6am; a human still hits publish at 8, until you've seen enough good drafts to trust it further.
🎤 Say this"Think confident intern: brilliant, fast, occasionally makes things up with a straight face. Your job isn't to distrust it; it's to verify the links and keep your name meaning something."

6 · Where it goes wrong: the pitfalls

Goal: Name the failure modes so people recognize them in the wild. For each: the tell, then the defense.

🌀 Hallucination

Confident, plausible, and wrong: fabricated sources, quotes, or stats. (Newsletter: a TechCrunch link that 404s.)

Defense: ask for sources, verify, spot-check before you rely on it.

🗑 Bad data in

Garbage in, garbage out. Feed it a messy export or last year's price list and you get confident nonsense.

Defense: check your inputs first; clean the data before the agent touches it.

🔒 Not your data

Pasting confidential customer or proprietary info into a tool you don't control, or that may train on your inputs.

Defense: strip identifiers, match the tool to the sensitivity, know the data policy.

📅 Stale knowledge

The model's training has a cutoff date; it'll state old facts as current. Fatal for anything news-driven.

Defense: give it live sources/tools; ask it to flag anything time-sensitive.

🚀 Over-automation

Wiring an agent to act (send, pay, delete) before it's earned trust.

Defense: start co-pilot; schedule drafts, not sends; add an off-switch.

😴 Over-trust & skill fade

Rubber-stamping output and slowly losing your own edge in the work.

Defense: stay the editor; make sure you can still explain what it did.

💡 Memorable line"It's a confident intern with a faulty memory and no sense of what's confidential. Brilliant, but you sign the work."
🎤 Say this"Every one of these has bitten someone. None of them means 'don't use AI'; they mean 'use it with your eyes open.' That's the whole difference between a tool and a liability."
